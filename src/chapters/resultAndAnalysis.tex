\section{Simulation Environment Configuration}
\label{sec:simulation_env_config}

The \gls{dqn}-based traffic signal control system was implemented using a realistic simulation of the Thapathali intersection in \gls{sumo}. The environment was constructed using OpenStreetMap data and calibrated with \gls{od} patterns collected from field studies. As shown in Figure \ref{fig:simulation_environment}, the simulation represents the four major approaches: Maitighar, Kupondole, Tripureshwor, and Maternity, with proper lane configurations and traffic signal placements. The simulation incorporated diverse vehicle types using \gls{pcu} factors from empirical studies, and traffic demand was generated using the allocation algorithms detailed in Chapter 5. The \gls{dqn} agent underwent training over 300 episodes with an exploration rate decaying from 1.0 to 0.01, utilizing the Max-Pressure reward function to optimize congestion management.

\section{Training Convergence and Learning Trajectory}
\label{sec:training_convergence}

Figure \ref{fig:reward_function} shows the episode reward and epsilon decay curve over 300 training episodes. During early exploration (episodes 0--50), rewards were highly variable at around $\sim$5,500 to $\sim$6,000. A sharp improvement began after episode 50 as epsilon decayed and exploitation increased. The 10-episode moving average reward rose steadily from approximately $\sim$5,300 to 3,395.8 by episode 300, a $\sim$43\% net improvement. The upward trend throughout training confirms successful policy convergence, with the agent transitioning fully to exploitation by episode 300.

\section{Wait Time Reduction Analysis}
\label{sec:wait_time_reduction}

Figure \ref{fig:wait_time} compares hourly average wait times for the Fixed 60s controller and the \gls{dqn} agent (Episode 300) on Day 5. The fixed controller ranges from 29s to 50s with high volatility, while the \gls{dqn} consistently stays between 23s and 30s.

\noindent Key results:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Overall average: \textbf{+22.90\%} improvement across all hours
    \item Morning peak average (9--11 AM): \textbf{+8.26\%} improvement
    \item Evening Peak Average (4--6 PM): \textbf{+19.26\%} improvement
\end{itemize}

\section{Overall Performance Summary and Analysis}
\label{sec:overall_performance_summary}

The three metrics evaluated-average wait time, average queue length, and throughput efficiency ratio-collectively present a consistent and unambiguous picture: the \gls{dqn} agent outperforms the fixed 60-second controller across every hour of the simulation day and across every dimension of traffic performance.

\subsection{Consistency Across Metrics}
\label{subsec:consistency_metrics}
The convergence of all three metrics in favour of the \gls{dqn} policy is analytically significant. A controller that reduces wait time by redistributing green time aggressively toward one approach could, in principle, inflate queue lengths elsewhere. The simultaneous 22.90\% reduction in wait time, 30.06\% reduction in queue length, and 32.65\% improvement in throughput efficiency ratio confirms that no such trade-off occurred; the agent achieved system-wide improvement rather than localised optimisation at the cost of other movements.

\subsection{Throughput Efficiency Ratio Analysis}
\label{subsec:throughput_efficiency_ratio}
As shown in Figure \ref{fig:throughput_per_second_of_wait_time}, the throughput efficiency ratio measured as vehicles processed per second of cumulative wait time favoured the \gls{dqn} agent at every hour of the day, with the gap widening notably during peak and late off-peak periods. The overall 32.65\% improvement is the largest gain observed across all three metrics, which is particularly meaningful given that total vehicle throughput remained comparable between the two controllers. This confirms that the \gls{dqn} agent did not improve efficiency by processing more vehicles, but rather by moving the same number of vehicles through the intersection with substantially less cumulative delay. During the morning peak (9--11 AM), the efficiency ratio improved by 9.33\%, and during the evening peak (4--6 PM) by 24.23\%, mirroring the pattern observed in wait time and queue length. The widening gap during evening peak hours suggests that the \gls{dqn} policy is most differentiated from the fixed plan precisely when demand is heaviest and signal timing decisions carry the greatest consequence.

\subsection{Peak Hour Behaviour}
\label{subsec:peak_hour_behaviour}
During the morning peak (9--11 AM), improvements were relatively moderate across all metrics: 8.26\% in wait time, 21.42\% in queue length, and 9.33\% in throughput efficiency compared to the evening peak (4--6 PM), which yielded 19.26\%, 20.89\%, and 24.23\% improvements respectively. This asymmetry is consistent with the nature of the Max-Pressure reward function: the agent responds proportionally to pressure differentials across the four approaches. The evening peak at Thapathali tends to produce more uneven directional demand, giving the adaptive policy greater opportunity to outperform the fixed plan's uniform phase allocation.

\subsection{Variance Reduction}
\label{subsec:variance_reduction}
The fixed controller's wait time ranged from 29s to 50s across hours while the \gls{dqn} agent remained within 23--30s. Similarly, queue lengths under the fixed plan sustained 10--13 vehicles during peak hours versus 5.9--10.3 under \gls{dqn}. The throughput efficiency curve in Figure \ref{fig:throughput_per_second_of_wait_time} reflects this same stability; the \gls{dqn} ratio curve is not only higher but smoother than the F60 curve, indicating that the agent maintained consistent processing efficiency rather than producing sporadic bursts of high throughput separated by periods of congestion.

\subsection{Off-Peak Performance}
\label{subsec:off_peak_performance}
The \gls{dqn} agent's advantage extends into off-peak hours, with queue length falling to 1.83 vehicles by 22:00 compared to 5.0 under the fixed plan, and the throughput efficiency gap remaining visible even at low demand. This reflects the agent's ability to shorten unnecessary red phases when vehicle arrivals are sparse-astructural limitation of fixed-time control that allocates green time uniformly regardless of actual demand, thereby accumulating wait time even when queues are minimal.

Taken together, these results confirm that the \gls{dqn} policy learned a generalisable and robust signal control strategy, not merely one optimised for a narrow demand condition. The consistency of improvement across wait time, queue length, and throughput efficiency and the alignment of their peak-hour patterns indicates that all three metrics are capturing the same underlying phenomenon: the \gls{dqn} agent allocates green time more precisely in response to real-time intersection state, reducing both the duration and the downstream consequences of vehicle delay. The hour-by-hour breakdown in Table \ref{tab:wait_queue_comparison} supports this interpretation, showing no hour in which the fixed controller equals or surpasses the \gls{dqn} on any metric.
